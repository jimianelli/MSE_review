How do we decide how much fish to catch —
and how do we know if we're getting it right?

The science and honesty behind fishing limits — explained for everyone

A plain-language guide to fisheries science  |  For anyone who eats seafood or cares about the ocean

Opening notes: Welcome everyone. This talk is about something that affects every person who buys fish at the grocery store or orders it at a restaurant — and everyone who cares about ocean health. The question sounds simple: how many fish can we catch? But answering it honestly turns out to be a beautiful puzzle. Let's dig in.

We want healthy fish populations and thriving fishing communities — forever.

Fishing feeds hundreds of millions of people and supports countless coastal communities. We want it to keep doing that — not just this year, but for generations.

That means setting catch limits that are neither too high (risking collapse) nor too low (leaving fishing families without income).

Getting the balance right is genuinely hard — and genuinely important.

Fishing communities and ocean health both depend on getting catch limits right.

2 / 15
Notes: Start with the goal — not the problem. Both fishers and conservationists want the same fundamental thing: a productive ocean that keeps giving. The challenge is purely about how to measure and manage something we can't easily observe.

But here's the problem: you can't just count all the fish.

You can count trees in a forest. You can count cars in a parking lot. But fish live underwater, spread across vast areas of open ocean, and they move.

We only ever see a small, imperfect sample — from research vessels, from fishing boats' catch records, from underwater surveys.

The true number in the ocean is always an educated estimate.

This isn't a failure of science — it's just the nature of the problem.

Scientists see only a small fraction of the fish that are actually out there.

3 / 15
Notes: This is the fundamental challenge. We rely on sampling — trawl surveys, catch records, observer data — and then use mathematics to estimate the full population. It's like a doctor estimating a patient's health from a blood sample rather than examining every cell. The analogy helps make this feel like smart inference, not guesswork.

So scientists build a model — a mathematical picture of the fish population.

Think of it like a recipe that combines everything we know: survey samples, catch records, historical data, and biological knowledge about how the species grows and reproduces.

Feed in the ingredients, and the model outputs an estimate — how many fish are out there, and how fast they're replacing themselves.

All models are simplifications of reality. A model that included everything would be as complicated as the ocean itself.

Data goes in, an estimate of fish numbers and a catch limit come out.

4 / 15
Notes: The model is essentially a structured way to reason from partial evidence to a conclusion. It's similar to how economists build GDP models or climate scientists build climate models. The key point is that a model is a tool for thinking clearly — not a crystal ball.

Every model makes assumptions — and those assumptions flow directly into the catch limit.

To make the model work, scientists have to fill in things they can't directly measure — how fast the fish reproduce, how well young fish survive, how the population responds to fishing.

These aren't guesses — they're informed judgments. But they are choices. And different choices lead to different limits.

A single choice deep inside the model can cascade forward and change the catch limit by a surprising amount.

What scientists assume about the fish directly shapes how many we're allowed to catch.

5 / 15
Notes: This is the intellectual core of the whole presentation. The causal chain is tight and real: assumption → model output → catch limit. This isn't philosophical — it's mathematical. The assumption literally enters an equation and changes the answer. The audience doesn't need to understand the math to understand that the chain is real.

Some of the most important assumptions are ones we can't easily check.

One key assumption: how fast do young fish replace the ones that are caught? This turns out to be very difficult to measure — it requires decades of careful data that many fisheries don't have.

Scientists make their best estimate. But the honest range of possibilities is often wide.

Wide uncertainty here means wide uncertainty in the catch limit — even if the model looks precise.

The honest answer for some key inputs is a wide range, not a single precise number.

6 / 15
Notes: The "steepness" parameter in stock-recruitment relationships is the canonical example — it describes how much recruitment changes as spawning biomass changes. It's notoriously hard to estimate. Many fisheries use values borrowed from related species, or from published meta-analyses. The key point is that the data often genuinely cannot resolve this, and that's okay to say.

Same ocean, different assumption — very different catch limits.

Here's a concrete example of the stakes: take the same survey data, the same fishing records, the same ocean — but change one assumption about how fast young fish survive.

The model can return catch limits that differ by 30%, 50%, even more.

Both are defensible. But they'd lead to very different amounts of fishing.

This is not a sign that science has failed. It's a sign that we need to be honest about uncertainty when translating models into rules.

Changing one model assumption — with the same real-world data — can shift the catch recommendation by nearly half.

7 / 15
Notes: Real examples exist in Pacific groundfish, Atlantic cod, and many other stocks. The 30-50% range is not an exaggeration. What's important is that both answers can be scientifically defensible — the disagreement comes from genuine uncertainty, not incompetence. The question is whether the management system is robust to that disagreement.

The rules are designed to be cautious — but only if we know what we don't know.

Fisheries managers use a "more fish = can catch more; fewer fish = slow down" formula. It's built-in caution.

The formula also has a built-in safety buffer — the actual limit is set below the theoretical maximum, to account for uncertainty.

This works well — as long as we've correctly described how uncertain we really are.

If we understate our uncertainty when designing the buffer, the buffer becomes too small.

The "slow down when fish are scarce" rule works — but only if the safety buffer is set using the right amount of uncertainty.

8 / 15
Notes: The harvest control rule (HCR) is the formula that converts "how many fish are out there" into "how many we can catch this year." The buffer (the gap between the theoretical maximum and the actual limit) is meant to absorb uncertainty. But if the uncertainty was underestimated when the buffer was designed, the buffer is too small and won't protect adequately.

If we've underestimated our uncertainty, we may be fishing harder than we think — and the safety cushion isn't big enough.

Every catch limit comes with a stated risk: "there's less than a 50% chance we'll harm the population this year." But that statement depends on the model being right.

If the model's assumptions were overconfident, the real risk can be higher than what we said.

This is the core problem — not bad intentions, just honest overconfidence in a difficult model.

Honesty about uncertainty is not a weakness — it's the foundation of trustworthy science.

The gap between "what we said the risk was" and "what the risk actually was" comes from underestimated uncertainty.

Notes: This is what researchers call "probability of overfishing" — the chance that the real catch rate is above the sustainable maximum. When the operating model used to derive the harvest control rule doesn't include enough structural uncertainty, the HCR performs worse than advertised. Real simulations have shown this gap can double or triple the actual overfishing probability.

We can figure out which assumptions matter most — and focus our honesty there.

Not all uncertainties are equal. Some assumptions, even if slightly wrong, barely change the catch limit. Others — especially around reproduction — can flip the advice dramatically.

Scientists can rank these and be transparent about which ones deserve the most scrutiny.

This is progress: knowing what we don't know, and saying so clearly.

Transparency isn't just honest — it helps managers and the public understand where caution is most needed.

Some assumptions move the needle far more than others — those deserve extra scrutiny and transparency.

Notes: This is sensitivity analysis — testing which inputs drive the most variation in outputs. The ranking here is illustrative but realistic: recruitment/steepness and natural mortality (M) tend to dominate, while some other parameters have surprisingly little effect on the final limit. This is useful information for both science and management.

What if we could test our fishing rules in a simulated ocean — before using them for real?

That's exactly what fisheries scientists now do. They build a simulated version of the ocean — complete with all the messy reality they can model — and then run the fishing rule against it thousands of times.

This reveals: "Does this rule actually work across all the ways the ocean might really be?"

It's like a flight simulator for fisheries management — find the problems before lives depend on it.

Testing a fishing rule in a simulated ocean before using it for real is like stress-testing a bridge before opening it to traffic.

11 / 15
Notes: This is Management Strategy Evaluation (MSE). The "operating model" is the simulated ocean — it's meant to represent the range of ways the real ocean might actually be. The estimation model (what managers use) is separate from the operating model, and crucially, it doesn't have to be "right" — it just has to generate advice that leads to good outcomes. MSE lets us test that connection explicitly.

More fish now, or healthier stocks long-term? These goals don't always point the same way.

When we run the simulated ocean, we can see: some fishing rules deliver higher catches in the short run but leave the population more fragile. Others protect the population well but yield less fish each year.

This is a genuine trade-off — there is no single "best" rule without first agreeing on what matters most.

Science can show the trade-offs clearly. But choosing between them is a values decision.

Science can map the trade-offs. But which trade-off to make is a question for society, not just scientists.

12 / 15
Notes: The "performance frontier" or Pareto frontier concept: any point on the curve is efficient — you can't improve one objective without harming another. Points inside the curve are waste (achieving less than possible). Points above the curve are impossible. The key insight: science tells us where the curve is; values tell us where on it we want to be.

Those trade-offs should be made openly — with input from everyone who has a stake.

Fishing rules affect fishing families, coastal communities, seafood buyers, and anyone who cares about ocean ecosystems. These groups sometimes have different priorities — and those differences are legitimate.

The role of science is to show the trade-offs clearly. The role of governance is to make the choice transparently, with everyone at the table.

Decisions that affect everyone should be made by everyone — not just the people with the most technical knowledge.

Everyone with a stake in the fishery — fishers, scientists, conservationists, communities, seafood buyers — deserves a seat at the table.

13 / 15
Notes: This is about the governance dimension. MSE is most powerful when stakeholders help define the performance metrics and the scenarios tested. When the public helps set the objectives, the resulting rule has more legitimacy and is more likely to be followed. Science informs the conversation; it doesn't replace it.

The science is getting better at showing trade-offs clearly. But using these tools consistently still needs work.

The old approach: "Here's the number. Trust us." The new approach shows a range of outcomes and trade-offs honestly.

The tools exist. But making sure every fishery actually uses them — and that results are communicated accessibly — requires ongoing commitment from scientists, managers, and the public.

Better science, consistently applied, is within reach. It's a goal worth pushing for.

The shift from "one number, trust us" to "here are the trade-offs across many scenarios" represents genuine scientific progress.

14 / 15
Notes: The governance challenge is real. MSE is computationally intensive and requires defining objectives upfront — which requires stakeholder engagement before the analysis, not just review of results afterward. Many fisheries still don't use MSE. Where it is used, it's often not communicated to the public. Closing that gap is the unfinished business.

Getting this right matters — for the fish, for fishing families, and for everyone who eats seafood.

The science of fisheries management is genuinely hard — and genuinely improving. Honest models, transparent trade-offs, and open governance aren't just good science. They're the foundation of fisheries that can feed the world for generations.

We don't always know everything. But we're getting better at knowing what we don't know — and saying so.
