FRAMING

How Assessment Assumptions
Propagate into Management Advice

Diagnosing and managing structural risk in stock assessment

Fisheries Management & MSE Symposium  |  2026

Speaker notes: Opening framing. The central question is not whether assessments are "right" but how structural choices — model form, priors, data weighting — shape the advice we deliver. This talk traces that causal chain and asks what we can do about it.
1 / 15
GOAL

We want management advice that is stable, precautionary, and defensible.

The institutional goal is clear: provide scientific advice that keeps harvest within safe biological limits while supporting economic objectives.

Stability matters because industry plans around it. Precaution matters because stock collapses are asymmetric and slow to recover.

Limit reference point → Buffer (ABC < OFL) → Harvest decision

Schematic HCR: harvest rate is zero below Bₗᵟₘ, ramps linearly to Fₘₓ𝑦 between Bₗᵟₘ and Bₜₐᵟ, and is held constant above Bₜₐᵟ. ABC is set below OFL; the buffer width depends on how fully uncertainty is characterized.

Speaker notes: This is the normative baseline — what we are trying to achieve. The HCR is the mechanism that is supposed to translate assessment output into precautionary advice. The buffer between OFL and ABC is explicitly designed to absorb uncertainty. The question this talk addresses is: does it actually absorb the right kind of uncertainty?

BUT

BUT every assessment embeds structural assumptions that are not in the data.

Model structure — growth form, selectivity shape, stock-recruit relationship, natural mortality — is chosen by the analyst, not estimated from data.

These choices are prior commitments that constrain what the likelihood surface can express.

ABC = f( B̂, F̂ₘₓ𝑦, σ̂ )
where B̂ ← model structure + data

Structural assumptions feed through the model before any likelihood is evaluated. They shape what the data can and cannot reveal.

Speaker notes: The point is not that these assumptions are arbitrary — they often reflect genuine scientific knowledge. But they are commitments. When they are wrong, the model cannot self-correct because the likelihood surface is defined over parameters given a fixed structure. Misspecification is invisible to within-model diagnostics.
3 / 15
THEREFORE

THEREFORE management advice inherits every structural assumption.

The causal path from assumption to advice is direct and often unbroken. Reference points, catch limits, and rebuilding timelines all trace back to model structure.

The system cannot produce advice that contradicts its own structural priors.

Directed acyclic graph (DAG): structural assumptions (red) propagate through estimated quantities to determine every downstream management product. There is no "assumption-free" path to the advice.

Speaker notes: This DAG is the core causal diagram for the talk. Red nodes are inputs the data cannot fully constrain. Blue node is the ultimate output. The entire system is downstream of structural choices made before data are fitted. This is the mechanism — not a critique, but an architectural fact.
4 / 15
BUT

BUT many structural assumptions are weakly informed by available data.

Steepness (h) of the stock-recruit relationship is rarely estimable from a single time series. Natural mortality (M) is almost never directly observed.

Weak identifiability means the likelihood surface is flat over a wide range of parameter values — the data are consistent with many hypotheses.

∂ℓ/∂h ≈ 0   over h ∈ [0.5, 0.95]
— typical for short, noisy time series

Schematic negative log-likelihood profile for steepness. The MLE is a single point on a nearly flat surface — small changes in h produce very different population dynamics.

Speaker notes: Steepness and M are the canonical examples, but selectivity and catchability trends face similar problems. When the likelihood surface is flat, the MLE is not meaningfully different from many alternative values. The prior — explicit or implicit — is doing the real work.

THEREFORE

THEREFORE uncertainty propagates nonlinearly through the assessment.

Small differences in assumed recruitment steepness produce large differences in estimated Bₘₓ𝑦 and, therefore, in the calculated ABC.

The relationship is nonlinear: uncertainty does not cancel — it compounds across the model structure.

ABC ∝ OFL · (1 − P[F > Fₘₓ𝑦])
OFL = Fₘₓ𝑦(h) · Bₘₓ𝑦(h) — both depend on h

Stylized relationship: a 0.2-unit change in assumed steepness can shift the calculated ABC by 40–60% via its effect on both Fₘₓ𝑦 and Bₘₓ𝑦. Shaded band reflects parameter uncertainty within each h assumption.

Speaker notes: This is the core empirical claim. It is not hypothetical — it has been documented in real assessments (e.g., Pacific groundfish). The ABC difference between h=0.6 and h=0.8 is not within-model estimation uncertainty — it is structural uncertainty that the standard assessment framework does not acknowledge. The buffer between OFL and ABC was not designed to absorb this.

BUT

BUT management frameworks typically treat point estimates as sufficient statistics.

The HCR receives a single B̂ and a single F̂ₘₓ𝑦. Structural uncertainty — across model configurations — is not formally represented.

The ABC buffer is calibrated to within-model parameter uncertainty (σ), not to cross-model structural divergence.

One model selected → Point estimate B̂ → Single OFL → P* buffer

Structural uncertainty enters the HCR as zero.

The distribution actually used in setting the ABC buffer (blue) is systematically narrower than the true distribution (red) that includes structural model uncertainty.

Speaker notes: The P* approach (PFMC, NPFMC) is sophisticated within its own logic — it correctly accounts for within-model estimation uncertainty. But it takes the model structure as given. If the true structural uncertainty is represented by the red curve, the buffer is set based on the blue curve. The implication is systematic underestimation of risk.

THEREFORE

THEREFORE risk is mischaracterized: the stated probability of overfishing is optimistic.

If structural assumptions are systematically biased and unaccounted, the realized probability of exceeding Fₘₓ𝑦 will exceed the nominal P* target.

The management system is calibrated to a model, not to the stock. Deviations between model and reality accumulate silently.

P[Factual > Fₘₓ𝑦] ≫ P*
when structural bias is present and unmodeled

Schematic: when structural bias is unmodeled, realized overfishing probability (red) exceeds the nominal P* target (blue dashed), with the gap representing systematic rather than random error.

Speaker notes: This is not a theoretical concern. MSE studies (e.g., Punt et al. 2016, Wiedenmann et al. 2017) have demonstrated that real management systems applied to simulated populations with misspecified models can have realized overfishing rates far above the nominal target. The gap is not random — it has a structural origin.

BUT

BUT we can make causal structure explicit — which makes assumption sensitivity diagnosable.

A formal causal model — whether a DAG or a structured sensitivity analysis — externalizes which assumptions drive which outputs.

This converts implicit choices into testable hypotheses about the data-generating process.

Assumption A → Output X
Assumption B → Output X ← testable
Assumption C → Output Y

Extended causal DAG. Orange dashed boxes identify nodes with high sensitivity — where the assessment output is most responsive to assumption changes. These are diagnostic priorities.

Speaker notes: Making the DAG explicit serves two functions: (1) it identifies which assumptions to investigate through sensitivity analysis, and (2) it provides a defensible, documented record of the causal claims embedded in the assessment. Transparency about assumptions is a precondition for evaluating them.
9 / 15
THEREFORE

THEREFORE sensitivity analysis can locate where assumptions drive advice most strongly.

A structured sensitivity table shows which assumptions produce the largest proportional change in OFL relative to the base case.

This prioritizes where additional data, research, or model averaging would reduce risk most effectively.

Assumptions that are both highly influential and weakly constrained represent the greatest unmodeled risk.

Stylized tornado diagram: bars show OFL response to low/high alternative assumptions. Steepness and M dominate — they should be the focus of data collection and model averaging efforts.

Speaker notes: Tornado diagrams of this kind should be standard reporting in stock assessments. They are not exotic — they are straightforward to produce and they immediately communicate to decision-makers where the assessment is most vulnerable. The goal is not to produce a single "best" sensitivity but to reveal the architecture of uncertainty.
10 / 15
BUT

BUT sensitivity analysis alone cannot tell us whether a management strategy performs acceptably.

A sensitivity table shows how advice would change under alternative assumptions. It does not show whether the resulting management strategy would achieve its objectives over time.

Performance is path-dependent: it emerges from the feedback loop between assessment, advice, and the stock's true dynamics.

Sensitivity: static ≠ MSE: closed-loop

The stock does not observe our model. It responds to harvest, not to our assumptions about it.

MSE closes the loop: harvest from the (possibly misspecified) assessment model acts on the true population (OM) and performance is measured against explicit objectives. Sensitivity analysis alone cannot produce this.

Speaker notes: The distinction between open-loop (sensitivity) and closed-loop (MSE) is fundamental. In an open-loop analysis, you hold the stock at a fixed state and ask how advice changes. In a closed-loop MSE, the stock evolves in response to the harvest generated by potentially misspecified advice, and you measure actual performance outcomes.

THEREFORE

THEREFORE MSE tests strategy performance across the space of plausible operating models.

By running the closed-loop simulation under many OMs — including structurally different hypotheses — MSE characterizes how robust a strategy is to assumption uncertainty.

Robustness: acceptable performance across the range of plausible realities, not just under the base-case assumption.

Performance scorecard: HCR-B (dashed outline) maintains acceptable P[B > Bₗᵟₘ] across all four operating models, while HCR-D fails under three. Robustness is the criterion, not base-case performance alone.

Speaker notes: This is the key output of an MSE: not a single number but a performance profile across OMs. The OM set should be constructed to span the range of structural uncertainty identified in the sensitivity analysis — this is where slides 9 and 12 connect. The criterion for strategy selection shifts from "best under the base case" to "acceptable under all plausible cases."
12 / 15
BUT

BUT management objectives conflict: conservation performance trades off against yield and stability.

No single HCR simultaneously maximizes long-run yield, minimizes collapse risk, and minimizes interannual variation in catch.

These are genuine trade-offs, not failures of optimization. The efficient frontier reveals what is achievable; it cannot choose a point on it.

That choice is a governance decision — informed by, but not determined by, the MSE.

Trade-off space: each point is a harvest strategy evaluated by MSE. The Pareto frontier defines what is achievable; strategies off it are dominated. Position on the frontier is a value judgment, not a scientific one.

Speaker notes: This is the clearest way to visualize why MSE is a governance tool and not just a technical one. Once you have mapped the efficient frontier, science has done its job. The choice of where to operate on that frontier — how much conservation performance to trade for yield stability — requires explicit stakeholder input. This is where the MSE process connects to fishery management councils and their mandate.
13 / 15
BUT

BUT transparency about assumptions and trade-offs is uneven across assessments and jurisdictions.

Many operational assessments report a base-case model and a few sensitivity runs without quantifying the contribution of structural uncertainty to the ABC range.

Trade-off analysis is often absent from formal advice documents, leaving councils without the information needed to make defensible allocation decisions.

Unstated assumptions carry the same causal weight as stated ones.

Reporting gap: the items most absent from standard assessment documents (dashed) are those most responsible for advice uncertainty. The gap is not technical — it reflects norms and capacity.

Speaker notes: This is a structural governance problem. Most scientific assessment teams are under-resourced relative to the complexity of the reporting task. Standardizing the reporting of sensitivity and OM-based uncertainty would require investment in tools, templates, and review capacity. The ICES Transparent Assessment Framework and some NPFMC/PFMC initiatives are moving in this direction, but progress is uneven.
14 / 15
THEREFORE

THEREFORE governance must integrate quantitative diagnostics — not as optional addenda but as standard products.

If structural assumption uncertainty is the primary driver of advice risk, it must be quantified and reported with the same rigor as within-model estimation uncertainty.

This requires: formal OM sets, standardized sensitivity reporting, MSE-informed HCR evaluation, and explicit trade-off documentation for councils.

Explicit OM set → Structural uncertainty quantified
Closed-loop MSE → Performance under uncertainty known
Trade-off frontier → Governance decision is defensible

The integrated workflow converts implicit structural risk into explicit, quantified uncertainty — enabling governance decisions that are transparent about what is known, what is assumed, and what is traded off.

Speaker notes: The conclusion is not that we need more research before we can act — we need to reorganize existing research products into a more transparent and decision-relevant format. DAGs, sensitivity analysis, OM sets, and MSE are not new methods. What is needed is their institutionalization as standard assessment deliverables, with review criteria that explicitly evaluate structural uncertainty reporting. This is a governance reform as much as a scientific one.
